Food Tourism in South Africa

South Africa's food scene is as diverse and vibrant as its people. Shaped by indigenous African traditions, Cape Malay heritage, Dutch and British colonial influences, and the flavours of Indian and Asian communities, the country's cuisine is a rich and delicious tapestry of tastes. Food tourism in South Africa offers travellers an extraordinary culinary journey — from bustling street food markets and traditional braais to fine dining restaurants and world-renowned wine pairings.

  • The Braai — South Africa's Culinary Tradition
    • The braai — a wood or charcoal fire barbecue — is far more than a cooking method in South Africa; it is a deeply cherished cultural institution that brings people together across all backgrounds. Boerewors (spiced sausage), sosaties (marinated kebabs), and pap (maize porridge) are braai staples enjoyed from the Cape to Limpopo, and no visit to South Africa is complete without experiencing one.
  • Cape Malay Cuisine
    • Cape Malay cuisine is one of South Africa's most distinctive and celebrated culinary traditions, originating from the enslaved people brought to the Cape from Malaysia, Indonesia, and other parts of Asia in the 17th and 18th centuries. Fragrant dishes such as bobotie (spiced minced meat with egg custard topping), denningvleis, and koeksisters reflect a unique fusion of Eastern spices and African ingredients.
  • Street Food & Food Markets
    • South Africa's food markets are lively, colourful celebrations of the country's culinary diversity. The Neighbourgoods Market in Johannesburg's Braamfontein, the Oranjezicht City Farm Market in Cape Town, and the BAT Centre Food Market in Durban are beloved destinations where visitors can sample everything from bunny chow and vetkoek to artisan cheeses, craft beers, and freshly baked breads.
  • Fine Dining & Restaurant Scene
    • South Africa boasts a thriving fine dining scene with several restaurants consistently ranked among the best in the world. The Test Kitchen in Cape Town, helmed by chef Luke Dale-Roberts, has repeatedly been named one of the top 50 restaurants globally. The country's chefs draw on local ingredients — from Karoo lamb and West Coast seafood to indigenous herbs and Kalahari truffles — to create extraordinary dining experiences.
  • Durban Indian Cuisine & Bunny Chow
    • Durban is renowned for having some of the finest Indian food outside of India, a legacy of the Indian community that arrived in KwaZulu-Natal as indentured labourers in the 19th century. The city's most iconic dish is the bunny chow — a hollowed-out loaf of bread filled with rich, spicy curry — which has become one of South Africa's most beloved street foods and a symbol of Durban's vibrant food culture.
  • Franschhoek — Culinary Capital of South Africa
    • Franschhoek in the Cape Winelands is widely regarded as the culinary capital of South Africa. This charming valley town is lined with award-winning restaurants, artisan bakeries, chocolate shops, and gourmet delicatessens. Visitors can indulge in multi-course wine-paired menus at celebrated estates, explore the annual Franschhoek Bastille Festival, and discover why this small town punches far above its weight on the global food stage.
